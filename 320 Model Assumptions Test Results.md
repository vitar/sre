# Model Assumptions Test Results

## Scope and Method

This document executes the full sequence defined in **310 Model Assumptions Test Plan** against the current reference set files.

Execution method: adversarial analytical testing with cross-domain counterexample pressure (biological, technical/computational, organizational/economic, social/political, multi-unit work systems, and shared-system/portfolio contexts).

Each record follows the plan template:

- claim,
- failure condition,
- strongest counterexample class,
- evidence/reasoning,
- rescue threshold,
- outcome,
- revision action,
- affected documents.

---

## Executive Outcome

- **Foundational layer:** survives with reinterpretation/layering pressure at mediator and complexity edges.
- **Empirical layer:** survives with narrowing on strength of generalization claims.
- **Operational layer:** survives with narrowing; reliability depends on artifacts, proxies, and declaration discipline.
- **Framing layer:** survives with narrowing/layering at open-boundary and multi-unit extremes.
- **Overall posture:** core is retained; application is conditional; maturity depends on disciplined instrumentation and explicit layering.

### Highest-Risk Pressure Points (post-execution)

1. **A1/F1 Three-contour sufficiency** in governance/meaning-heavy social systems.
2. **A2/F6 Mediator-not-contour treatment** under persistent power/legitimacy dominance.
3. **A5/F4 State by distribution** when allocation is opaque or symbolically mediated.
4. **C3/O3 Proxy-supported inference** where proxies are weak, delayed, or gameable.
5. **D3/C3 Multi-unit as extension** in strongly coupled, asynchronous, failure-propagating networks.

## Assumption Records

## A. Foundational Assumptions

### A1 — Three-contour sufficiency (F1)

- **Claim:** Survival, Reproduction, and Evolution are sufficient as the minimum irreducible core.
- **Failure condition:** A fourth cross-domain function is repeatedly irreducible and cannot be treated as mechanism/mediator/constraint within the three.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Governance-legitimacy-power intensive social/political systems.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Fourth-candidate factors often operate by shaping allocation/coordination across the three contours rather than replacing them. Pressure remains strongest in symbolic/legitimacy regimes.
- **Rescue threshold:** Keep tri-contour core only with explicit mediator formalization and anti-proliferation discipline.
- **Outcome:** **Survives with reinterpretation**.
- **Revision action:** **Reinterpret**.
- **Affected documents:** `110 Core Model Definition.md`, `230 Shared System State.md`, `210 Inter-Unit Synchronization Logic.md`.

### A2 — Mediator-not-contour treatment (F6)

- **Claim:** Cross-cutting factors (governance, trust, legitimacy, power, coordination) are better modeled as mediators than as extra core contours.
- **Failure condition:** These factors repeatedly behave as independent, contour-like allocation poles that cannot be represented as mediating conditions.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** States/platforms/large institutions where control architecture appears primary.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Mediator treatment remains workable when mediators are modeled as allocation shapers with explicit directional effects. Ambiguity rises when mediators are left purely qualitative.
- **Rescue threshold:** Require mediator specification fields (type, channel, direction, strength, lag).
- **Outcome:** **Survives with layering**.
- **Revision action:** **Layer** (mediator instrumentation layer).
- **Affected documents:** `230 Shared System State.md`, `170 Application Artifact Suite.md`, `130 Metrics And Proxies.md`.

### A3 — Universal tri-contour relevance (F2)

- **Claim:** All three contours remain relevant in viable in-scope systems, even when hidden/displaced/externally compensated.
- **Failure condition:** Durable in-scope systems exist with a true non-displaceable absence of one contour.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Subsidized or externally buffered systems that appear to “outsource” one contour.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Apparent absences are usually displacement or delayed dependency (e.g., externalized Survival or imported Evolution).
- **Rescue threshold:** Require explicit displacement/externalization annotation before declaring contour absence.
- **Outcome:** **Survives**.
- **Revision action:** None.
- **Affected documents:** none.

### A4 — Constraint through allocation (F3)

- **Claim:** Allocation to one contour constrains allocations to others over relevant horizons.
- **Failure condition:** Broad durable classes show unconstrained multi-contour growth under meaningful scarcity.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Automation/AI leverage phases and high-capital expansion windows.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Local synergies exist, but constraints re-emerge via complexity, attention, governance, energy, and coordination limits.
- **Rescue threshold:** Keep claim horizon-qualified (short-run synergy, long-run constraint recurrence).
- **Outcome:** **Survives**.
- **Revision action:** None.
- **Affected documents:** none.

### A5 — State by distribution (F4)

- **Claim:** System state can be meaningfully described through contour distribution.
- **Failure condition:** Allocation cannot be estimated reliably enough for repeatable diagnosis/comparison.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Opaque institutions and symbolic systems with weak observability.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Qualitative state description is useful; quantitative confidence drops without agreed proxy sets and uncertainty treatment.
- **Rescue threshold:** Mandate proxy schema + confidence bands + evidence trace.
- **Outcome:** **Survives with narrowing**.
- **Revision action:** **Narrow**.
- **Affected documents:** `130 Metrics And Proxies.md`, `170 Application Artifact Suite.md`, `180 Comparability Rules.md`.

### A6 — Complexity without changed core (F5)

- **Claim:** Increasing complexity/nesting adds interactions without requiring new core geometry.
- **Failure condition:** Recurring complex cases require fundamentally different ontology rather than layered extension.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Multi-unit coupled systems with synchronization failures and shared-state cascade dynamics.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Core remains explanatory at local unit level; emergent behavior requires explicit interaction layers.
- **Rescue threshold:** Keep tri-contour core while formalizing inter-unit/shared-state layer contracts.
- **Outcome:** **Survives with layering**.
- **Revision action:** **Layer**.
- **Affected documents:** `210 Inter-Unit Synchronization Logic.md`, `220 Multi-Unit Failure and Breakdown.md`, `230 Shared System State.md`.

## B. Empirical Assumptions

### B1 — Dominance produces characteristic distortions (E1)

- **Claim:** Prolonged contour dominance tends to produce recurring distortions.
- **Failure condition:** Dominance patterns do not produce stable distortion signatures across domains.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Highly adaptive systems that rebalance quickly.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Distortion patterns recur (stagnation, brittle scaling, incoherent adaptation), but intensity and timing vary by mediator context.
- **Rescue threshold:** Treat distortions as probabilistic tendencies, not deterministic laws.
- **Outcome:** **Survives with narrowing**.
- **Revision action:** **Narrow**.
- **Affected documents:** `190 Model Laws.md`.

### B2 — No sustainable triple-maximization under scarcity (E2)

- **Claim:** Sustainable simultaneous maximization of all three contours does not occur under meaningful scarcity.
- **Failure condition:** Durable multi-cycle class demonstrates near-max across all three without deferred costs.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Dominant empires/platforms during long expansion periods.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Apparent triple-max windows are phase-limited and usually transfer costs across time or boundaries.
- **Rescue threshold:** Keep explicit definition of “sustainable” (multi-cycle, cost-internalized).
- **Outcome:** **Survives**.
- **Revision action:** None.
- **Affected documents:** none.

### B3 — Behavior often understandable via contours + mediators + feedback (E3)

- **Claim:** Deep system behavior can often be interpreted through allocation, mediators, and feedback loops.
- **Failure condition:** Model repeatedly fails to add explanatory traction over simpler alternatives.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Randomized/high-noise environments with weak structural persistence.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Explanatory traction is good in structured dynamic systems; weaker in highly stochastic or minimally bounded settings.
- **Rescue threshold:** Gate claim by system boundedness and signal quality.
- **Outcome:** **Survives with narrowing**.
- **Revision action:** **Narrow**.
- **Affected documents:** `160 Use Constraints and Discipline.md`.

### B4 — Failure anchor via viable continuity and functional identity (E4)

- **Claim:** Failure diagnosis anchored in viable continuity/functional identity is broadly appropriate.
- **Failure condition:** Alternative failure anchors consistently outperform across domains.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Mission-shifting institutions where identity is contested.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Anchor remains useful but requires explicit identity declaration and horizon handling.
- **Rescue threshold:** Require predeclared continuity/identity criteria per analysis.
- **Outcome:** **Survives with reinterpretation**.
- **Revision action:** **Reinterpret**.
- **Affected documents:** `140 Failure and Breakdown Definition.md`, `120 Classification Protocol.md`.

## C. Operational Assumptions

### C1 — Primary intent classification is usually feasible (O1)

- **Claim:** Analysts can identify primary functional intent reliably enough for contour assignment.
- **Failure condition:** Persistent high inter-rater disagreement despite protocol use.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Mixed interventions with dual-purpose design (stability + adaptation).
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Reliability improves with intent-first and horizon-tag rules; degrades without artifact discipline.
- **Rescue threshold:** Require rationale trace and secondary-effects tagging.
- **Outcome:** **Survives with narrowing**.
- **Revision action:** **Narrow**.
- **Affected documents:** `120 Classification Protocol.md`, `170 Application Artifact Suite.md`.

### C2 — Mediator-first handling works operationally (O2)

- **Claim:** Mediator-first handling reduces forced misclassification in cross-cutting objects.
- **Failure condition:** Mediator usage increases ambiguity more than it reduces distortion.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Analyses with many interacting mediators and weak evidence.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Works when mediators are explicitly typed and linked to allocation effects; fails when mediator labels are generic.
- **Rescue threshold:** Add minimal mediator evidence standard.
- **Outcome:** **Survives with layering**.
- **Revision action:** **Layer**.
- **Affected documents:** `170 Application Artifact Suite.md`, `130 Metrics And Proxies.md`.

### C3 — Proxy-supported inference is usually good enough (O3)

- **Claim:** Proxies can credibly estimate contour posture where direct allocation is hidden.
- **Failure condition:** Proxy-to-allocation mapping is unstable, manipulable, or weakly correlated.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Political/organizational systems with strategic reporting and lagged indicators.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Proxies help materially when triangulated; single-proxy interpretations are fragile.
- **Rescue threshold:** Require multi-proxy triangulation with confidence levels and drift checks.
- **Outcome:** **Survives with narrowing**.
- **Revision action:** **Narrow**.
- **Affected documents:** `130 Metrics And Proxies.md`, `180 Comparability Rules.md`.

### C4 — Boundary and horizon declarations discipline interpretation (O4)

- **Claim:** Explicit boundaries/horizons reduce interpretive drift.
- **Failure condition:** Declarations do not improve reproducibility.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Highly contested systems where boundaries are politicized.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Discipline improves when declarations are mandatory and comparable; optional declarations provide limited benefit.
- **Rescue threshold:** Move boundary/horizon declaration from recommendation to required artifact field.
- **Outcome:** **Survives**.
- **Revision action:** None.
- **Affected documents:** none.

### C5 — Conditional comparability is achievable (O5)

- **Claim:** Comparable analysis is possible when artifacts and assumptions are aligned.
- **Failure condition:** Alignment requirements are too strict to achieve in practice.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Cross-domain comparisons with incompatible proxy structures.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Comparability is feasible in bands (high/medium/low) rather than binary yes/no.
- **Rescue threshold:** Use comparability tiers with explicit residual uncertainty.
- **Outcome:** **Survives with reinterpretation**.
- **Revision action:** **Reinterpret**.
- **Affected documents:** `180 Comparability Rules.md`.

### C6 — Discipline and artifact traceability improve reliability (O6)

- **Claim:** Better process discipline materially improves repeatability and reduces misuse.
- **Failure condition:** Structured artifacts do not improve analyst agreement or error detection.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Rapid-response environments where teams bypass protocol.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Reliability improves when artifact completion is enforced and auditable; benefits shrink when process is partial.
- **Rescue threshold:** Define minimum viable artifact set for fast contexts.
- **Outcome:** **Survives**.
- **Revision action:** None.
- **Affected documents:** none.

## D. Framing Assumptions

### D1 — Relevant phenomena can be treated as bounded dynamic systems (C1)

- **Claim:** Target phenomena are usually boundable enough for structured use.
- **Failure condition:** Boundary instability routinely prevents repeatable inference.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Diffuse transnational publics and open memetic ecosystems.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Many practical slices are analyzable; full open systems require explicit scope reduction.
- **Rescue threshold:** Require declared unit, nesting level, and excluded externalities.
- **Outcome:** **Survives with narrowing**.
- **Revision action:** **Narrow**.
- **Affected documents:** `160 Use Constraints and Discipline.md`, `170 Application Artifact Suite.md`.

### D2 — Scarcity and competing demands remain structurally important (C2)

- **Claim:** Scarcity/trade-offs remain central in intended domains.
- **Failure condition:** Intended domains are mostly abundance-like with weak competition constraints.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Near-zero marginal cost digital replication contexts.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Scarcity often shifts to attention, legitimacy, coordination, latency, compliance, or energy.
- **Rescue threshold:** Require declaration of scarcity type (material, cognitive, institutional, temporal).
- **Outcome:** **Survives**.
- **Revision action:** None.
- **Affected documents:** none.

### D3 — Multi-unit/shared-system layers are extensions of same core (C3)

- **Claim:** Multi-unit synchronization and shared-state logic can be layered on tri-contour core.
- **Failure condition:** Recurring multi-unit behaviors require separate ontology with different primitives.
- **Strongest counterexample class:** Strongly coupled portfolios with asynchronous objectives and cascading failures.
- **Evidence/reasoning:** Unit-level contour logic remains usable; system-level failure propagation needs explicit extension semantics.
- **Rescue threshold:** Formalize extension contracts (unit state, coupling type, propagation pathway, recovery modes).
- **Outcome:** **Survives with layering**.
- **Revision action:** **Layer**.
- **Affected documents:** `210 Inter-Unit Synchronization Logic.md`, `220 Multi-Unit Failure and Breakdown.md`, `230 Shared System State.md`.
