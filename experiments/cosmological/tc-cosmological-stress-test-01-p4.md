## Dark Matter Types as Mediator Configurations

Now the more productive question. If dark matter is a mediator, the cold/warm/wave debate is a debate about **how the mediator configures Gates**. Each dark matter type produces a different Gate architecture.

### Cold Dark Matter → Sharp, Localized Gates

Cold dark matter particles are heavy and slow-moving. They collapse into compact, dense halos with steep gravitational potential wells. The resulting Gate architecture is:

- **High selectivity, small aperture.** Matter flows into deep, narrow wells. What enters a halo stays — the Gate is effectively one-way for most baryonic matter. Structures that form are compact and concentrated.
- **Hierarchical nesting.** Small halos form first, then merge into larger ones. The Gate structure is hierarchically nested — many small Gates within larger Gates. This produces abundant substructure (dwarf galaxies within galaxy halos, galaxy groups within cluster halos).
- **Sharp boundaries.** The transition between "inside the halo" and "outside the halo" is relatively steep. The Gate has a defined edge. Matter is either captured or not — the intermediate zone is narrow.

**TC diagnostic implication:** Cold dark matter mediators produce systems with strong internal Gate structure, high selectivity, and hierarchical decomposition. In organizational terms, this resembles a governance structure with many nested approval layers, clear jurisdictional boundaries, and high capture — once resources enter a division, they rarely leave.

**Allocation consequences:** The sharp, hierarchical Gate architecture channels baryonic matter efficiently into compact structures (high Reproduction efficiency within structures) but creates isolation between structures (low cross-structure Evolution — halos don't easily share material or information). The prediction: cold-mediated systems are good at local Reproduction, poor at system-wide Evolution.

### Warm Dark Matter → Smooth, Distributed Gates

Warm dark matter particles are lighter and faster-moving. Their thermal velocity prevents them from collapsing into small structures — small-scale gravitational wells are "washed out." The resulting Gate architecture is:

- **Lower selectivity, broader aperture.** Matter flows along broad filaments rather than falling into compact wells. The Gate is more permeable — matter can flow through and along structures rather than being captured.
- **Suppressed small-scale structure.** The minimum halo size is larger. Below a characteristic scale, no Gates form at all — matter flows freely. The Gate architecture has a resolution floor.
- **Gradual boundaries.** The transition between inside and outside is gradual. The Gate is a gradient, not a wall. Matter experiences varying degrees of gravitational influence across a wide zone.

**TC diagnostic implication:** Warm dark matter mediators produce systems with fewer, larger Gates, lower selectivity, and smoother flow. In organizational terms: fewer but broader governance structures, less jurisdictional sharpness, more cross-boundary flow. Resources move more freely between units.

**Allocation consequences:** Broader Gates produce more distributed Reproduction (structures form along filaments, not just in compact nodes) and potentially higher system-wide Evolution (material and information flow more freely between structures). But lower selectivity also means less efficient local concentration — structures may be less massive individually. The prediction: warm-mediated systems are better at distributed, diffuse Reproduction and cross-structure Evolution, but produce less concentrated local output.

### Wave Dark Matter → Oscillating, Interference-Pattern Gates

Wave dark matter (also called fuzzy dark matter) consists of ultralight particles with de Broglie wavelengths on galactic scales. The particles behave quantum-mechanically at macroscopic scales, producing interference patterns. The resulting Gate architecture is:

- **Interference-pattern selectivity.** Gates are not fixed structures — they are standing wave patterns that oscillate. Matter flows are shaped by constructive and destructive interference in the dark matter density field. Some regions are reinforced (constructive — Gates open), others are cancelled (destructive — Gates closed). The Gate map is a wave pattern, not a topographic map.
- **Dynamic reconfiguration.** Because the pattern is wave-based, it shifts over time. Gates open and close as interference patterns evolve. The mediator architecture is not static — it reconfigures continuously at a rate determined by the particle mass (lighter particles → longer wavelengths → slower oscillation → more stable Gates; heavier particles → shorter wavelengths → faster oscillation → more dynamic Gates).
- **Solitonic cores.** At the center of each halo, a stable standing wave (soliton) forms — a fixed Gate that does not oscillate. Surrounding the core, the interference pattern produces dynamic, fluctuating Gate behavior.

**TC diagnostic implication:** Wave dark matter mediators produce systems with a stable core Gate (solitonic center) surrounded by dynamically reconfiguring peripheral Gates (interference patterns). In organizational terms: a stable governance core with a fluid, oscillating periphery. Decision-making at the center is consistent; at the edges, rules shift and flow. Resources experience different Gate behavior depending on their distance from the core.

**Allocation consequences:** The oscillating peripheral Gates create periodic windows where matter can flow in or out of structures — not permanently open (warm) or permanently channeled (cold), but rhythmically permeable. This produces a distinctive temporal signature: Reproduction occurs in pulses synchronized with Gate oscillation, not continuously (warm) or on-capture-only (cold). Evolution is possible during open-Gate phases and suppressed during closed-Gate phases. The prediction: wave-mediated systems produce pulsatile, rhythmic allocation patterns with phase-dependent behavior — not the steady flow of warm or the hierarchical capture of cold.
